[Pro Forecasting] How causally impactful would the listed indicator be depending on 25th or 75th percentile resolution?

Please rate the following indicators on a scale of 1 to 99 following the criteria below, assessing how causally impactful the indicator is on causing a change in the US light-duty ZEV market share in 2030 if the indicator were to resolve in 2025 at the communitys 75th percentile forecast, as opposed to resolving at the communitys 25th percentile forecast:

  • A rating of 1 indicates that the indicator resolving at the 75th percentile value instead of the 25th percentile value in 2025 would have a massive and extremely important negative impact on light-duty ZEV market share in 2030. In other words, the ZEV market share would be lower than you otherwise forecasted if this were to occur, and you believe this indicator is the single most causally important factor affecting ZEV market share in 2030 by far.
  • A rating of 50 indicates that the indicator resolving at the 75th percentile value instead of the 25th percentile value in 2025 would neither positively or negatively impact ZEV market share in 2030. In other words, the ZEV market share would be unlikely to change from what you otherwise forecasted if this were to occur, and you believe this indicator is not at all a causally important factor affecting ZEV market share in 2030.
  • A rating of 99 indicates that the indicator resolving at the 75th percentile value instead of the 25th percentile value in 2025 would have a massive and extremely important positive impact on light-duty ZEV market share in 2030. In other words, the ZEV market share would be much higher than you otherwise forecasted if this were to occur, and you believe this indicator is the single most causally important factor affecting ZEV market share in 2030 by far.

Note that we are concerned with the impact of the indicator resolving at the 75th percentile as opposed to the 25th percentile. We are interested in learning your perceived causal impact of the range of outcomes associated with the interquartile range, so when assessing the causal impact please consider the world where the indicator resolves at the 75th percentile in comparison to the world where it resolves at the 25th percentile.

We are defining causal impact to mean the impact that can be directly attributed to this indicator alone. For example, if one indicator is likely to be heavily influenced by another listed or unlisted indicator but also will be influential on US light-duty ZEV market share, it should not be rated as if it were causally impactful and instead the indicator that is the underlying influence should be rated higher.

Please describe your ratings in the comments, and also note any confounding or reverse causality issues you believe to be at play.

Just to elaborate on what I meant in reference to supply chains for minerals: the main concern I have is that the major resource producing and exporting countries may either unintentionally or intentionally end up in a position where their exports are significantly reduced. Unintentional reductions in resource flows could be the result of civil or interstate conflicts; or could be the unintended result of government policy, like nationalisations, expropriations, and unexpected and significant increases in mining royalties, which discourage foreign investment.

Bolivia is an example of a country where I think government policy has already unintentionally significantly diminished the potential for resource production. Mexico is currently in the process of nationalising lithium, and Chile nearly nationalised the industry last year. As the value of these resources grows, I think there is a high risk of government policy focusing on potential short-term political benefits from nationalisations and increases in taxes at the risk of harming industry over the longer term.

Intentional reductions in resource flows could be the result of political tension with the US or the mining industry; or a result of other major consuming countries (for example, China) securing supply exclusivity in exchange for some significant benefit, like security guarantees, substantial development assistance, debt relief, etc. I think this is a greater risk in some of the less-developed producer countries.

In the US context, I think it would be politically controversial to openly tie something like development assistance or a security guarantee to assurances from the governments of major resource producing countries that they would not nationalise the mining industry or impose high mining royalties; this would probably be seen on the left as a form of neo-colonial/imperial hegemony, so it would probably be tricky to pull off. Conversely, in less democratic countries, it seems like this kind of quid pro quo would be more feasible.

One particular thing that it would probably be desirable to avoid is the formation of an OPEC-like cartel of critical minerals producers. If production is coordinated among the major producers, I worry that prices may settle on a level that is high enough that high-performance EVs remain a premium option out of the reach of many consumers.

Indicators of greatest significance

Change in Energy Transition Metal Index, rating: 20

I think this is the single most important indicator we have considered, in that a large increase in raw materials costs, particularly if it is sustained over a long period of time, could seriously imperil the affordability of the kind of high-performance ZEVs that US consumers desire. If a ZEV with the performance characteristics that US consumers consider acceptable retains a large subsidy-adjusted purchase price premium relative to a comparable ICE vehicle, I expect that to have a very significant negative impact on ZEV purchases, as less affluent consumers will be hard-pressed to make the transition even if they are eager to do so. A minor increase in the cost of raw materials, on the order of 8%, would probably be offset by battery technology improvements such that it would not significantly affect affordability; but larger increases, on the order of 70%, would probably mean ZEV purchase prices are still substantially higher than those of ICE vehicles.

Public charging stations, rating: 77.5

I think this is the second most important indicator among those we have considered, and I assign it relatively high causal significance because I think that if the number of charging stations in existence in the near term is towards the higher end of our expectations, it means that certain barriers (like permitting, or protectionist sourcing requirements) to construction have probably been somewhat remediated; or that they were less of an impediment than had been expected. If these potential problems have been addressed or overcome rapidly, the charging availability anxiety problem likely becomes a less significant impediment to ZEV purchases on a shorter timescale. I think that anxiety is one of the bigger issues that need to be overcome in order for consumers to be confident that a ZEV can meet their needs, so I think this is a particularly important indicator.

EV median range under $40k, rating: 70

Approximately 300 miles of range per charge seems to be a significant tipping point for American consumers. If relatively affordable mass-market EVs have achieved a median range well in excess of that as early as 2025, the range anxiety problem should be largely overcome, which I would expect to have a significant positive effect on EV sales. I would rate this as more significant if the 25th percentile value were significantly further from that 300 mile target, but 272 isn't too far off, so I consider this indicator a bit less significant than charging availability, given the larger difference between the 25th and 75th percentiles there.

Gasoline price, rating: 70

I rate this as a highly significant indicator on the basis that I expect uncomfortably high gas prices, especially if sustained, and if consumers expect them to be sustained, to be a major factor that causes consumers to consider purchasing ZEVs - but I don't rate it as more significant than those mentioned above because I think the anxieties that consumers have about suitability (range, charging availability) and purchase affordability are significant enough that even if gas prices are uncomfortably high, they would be (if not addressed) significant barriers to ZEV purchase.

Indicators of moderate significance

US ZEV sales share (2026), rating: 65

I consider this moderately important in that it indicates higher enthusiasm than expected for ZEV purchases; however, I don't assign it a higher rating because I think this indicator is downstream of those mentioned above. A higher ZEV sales share is the result of success elsewhere; such as maintaining low and stable raw materials prices, building charging infrastructure, and improving ZEV technology to meet consumers' needs and wants.

ZEV tax credits claimed, rating: 60

My reasoning here is much the same as mentioned above for the US ZEV sales share.

Global ZEV sales share, rating: 60

My reasoning here is much the same as mentioned above for the US ZEV sales share.

States adopting ZEV mandates, rating: 60

I consider this a moderately important indicator because I think it's likely that states that adopt ZEV mandates are likely to be states that would have had a relatively faster transition to ZEVs even in the absence of any mandates; however, they would still probably accelerate the transition. A significant matter of uncertainty here is how stable these mandates would be; the fact that they are in place in 2025 would not necessarily mean that they will remain in force unaltered through 2030. Uncertainty around regulatory stability causes me not to rate this as more significant. Another factor is that the extent to which it is politically feasible to enact a ZEV mandate is itself a result of other outcomes, like ZEV affordability, consumer satisfaction with ZEVs on the market, and consumer confidence in available charging infrastructure. As I think states likely need those to be trending in the right direction if they are to enact ZEV mandates, I think the existence of the mandates themselves is more a result of other factors than a major causal factor in itself.

ZEV models in US under $40k, rating: 60

I consider this a moderately important indicator because greater consumer choice in the ZEV marketplace would generally be expected to have a positive effect on the ZEV sales share; however, I don't rate this as more significant because there is significant uncertainty around the performance characteristics of vehicles in this segment of the market. If a significant fraction of vehicles in this segment have performance characteristics that do not satisfy many consumers, a large number of low-selling models may not have a particularly significant effect on the ZEV sales share. To the extent that it may be the case that there are many models of high-selling ZEVs under $40k, I think that would mainly be the result of success in achieving a stable supply of reasonably priced raw materials, so I do not rate this indicator as highly significant, as it is largely the result of successful policy elsewhere.

Indicators of least significance

California ZEV sales share, rating: 55

I rate this as one of the less significant indicators because I think the Californian economy, consumer, and regulatory environment are unrepresentative of the US in general, and likely to remain that way for the foreseeable future. However, California is one of the larger vehicle marketplaces in the US, and as such I think a higher ZEV sales share earlier in California could still be indicative of a faster than expected transition nationally. However, I think that would be the result of other indicators, like the charging infrastructure buildout pace, affordability of raw materials, gas prices, et cetera, so I assign this indicator relatively low causal significance in its own right.

EV industry employment, rating: 55

I rate this as one of the less significant indicators primarily because I think it is a result of success elsewhere; however, I think it does potentially have some causal significance of its own in that federal politicians may be more likely to advocate and vote for ZEV-friendly measures if they have a significant number of workers in their districts who would benefit from them, and if companies employing these workers are lobbying for such measures.

CO2 emissions in the US, rating: 55

I rate this as one of the less significant indicators because while I do think it is true that a non-trivial fraction of consumers purchase ZEVs because of environmental concerns, I think they comprise a relatively small proportion of the marketplace, and are likely to be early adopters. In general, I think the vast majority of consumers are much more concerned with factors like affordability and performance than emissions. However, if emissions are somewhat higher than expected, this group of consumers highly concerned about emissions may become larger. The political situation is relatively unclear, in that if emissions are higher than expected, it seems likely to have been a result of policy failures or changes in policy that have de-emphasized or diminished the ZEV transition. I am not particularly confident about this one, and may revise to a slightly negative impact, or no impact.

Electricity price, rating: 47.5

I consider this one of the least significant indicators because the difference between the two values is very small. I think it would have a very minor negative effect on ZEV sales.

Fuel economy regulations, rating: 52.5

I consider this one of the least significant indicators because the difference between the two values is quite small. I think it would have a very minor positive effect on ZEV sales.

ZEV federal fleet procurements, rating: 51

I consider this the least significant indicator because federal fleet purchases are a very small proportion of US vehicle purchases, and a greater ZEV share of the federal fleet seems unlikely to lead to either a significant change in consumer purchasing behaviour or changes in government policy that accelerate ZEV adoption. I rate it as very slightly positive because I think greater exposure to ZEVs among government workers may increase the probability of those workers purchasing ZEVs for themselves, or advocating for their use.

California ZEV sales share: Californias ZEV sale share definitely plays a large part in the total US market due to its size, but I am unsure about its causal impact above and beyond its representation in the market.

Fuel economy regulations: While a metric like this would in general have the potential to direct consumer behaviour quite strongly, the small differences between the 25th and 75th percentiles make this metric less causally impactful than it otherwise would have been.

EV industry employment: I think that EV industry employment has high correlation with the outcome variable, but its causal force is pretty much fully absorbed by other factors, making this a causally ineffectual variable.

CO2 emissions in the US: A difference of over half a billion tons may spur other policy changes that then drive the outcome variable, which is why I think that this factor itself does not strongly contribute to the outcome, but still has an above-neutral causal efficacy rating.

Here's my quick take on each indicator:

  • EV Range: Range is certainly one of the most talked about aspects around EVs and I expect it will substantially impact the rate of their adoption. With an 80 mile difference between 25th and 75th percentiles, I'm rating this as one of the more important indicators, though the biggest difference will likely be for long-haul travel with medium-to-heavy-duty vehicles.
  • Charging Stations: A roughly 2x difference in public charging stations seems like it would be impactful, though again this would be more relevant for long-haul travel and dependent on how good EV ranges are. If most day-to-day charging is done privately at homes or workplaces, that might slightly lessen the need for these around major cities at least, though not by very much due to e.g. tourists and business travelers.
  • US ZEV Sales [2026]: Seeing as ZEV adoption will follow an S-curve and likely still be before or not far above the inflection point (50% adoption), how the exponential shapes up within the next 3 years seems quite important.
  • Electricity Price: The difference between 11 and 12 cents/kWh is pretty small, so the importance is mostly negligible here.
  • ZEV Tax Credits: While maybe not perfectly so, the 75th and 25th percentiles here seem to essentially split the possibilities between something like "Democrats in power; tax credits largely in place or expanded" and "Republicans in power; tax credits largely cut or abolished". Ranking just the tax credits by themselves however, I don't expect their impact to be particularly large.
  • Global ZEV Sales: A little less impactful than US-specific sales, but certainly creates pressure for US adoption to not slack as much if other countries are doing well. Economies of scale matter for lowering costs, as @Pro D mentioned.
  • ZEV Mandates: Pretty direct cause-and-effect here for ZEV sales, though the absence of mandates in states that were seriously considering them in the first place doesn't mean their actual EV adoption will slow down all that much anyway.
  • ZEV Federal Fleet: Taking just the indicator itself into account, without the underlying implications for e.g. which party is in charge (see ZEV Tax Credits), this seems... basically irrelevant?
  • Gasoline Price: "While consumers appear to be influenced by both gasoline and electricity prices when making adoption decisions, the influence of gasoline prices is roughly four to six times stronger, relative to operating costs, than is the electricity price." https://www.ucits.org/research-project/2020-12/
  • Metal Prices: With a 60% difference in raw materials costs, this would obviously have a substantial impact on overall ZEV production costs in 2030. If I had to pick two of the most impactful indicators overall (from the ones given), it would be this and gas prices.
  • ZEV Models: Having a nearly 2x wider range of affordable EV models to choose from ought to help if it results in increased healthy competition between car companies, though I'm admittedly uncertain on how big of an impact going from 15 to 27 models makes (as opposed to say, 5 to 10 models).
  • California ZEV Sales: I think this is only impactful in the sense that California is the biggest EV market within the US, not that high percentages of EV adoptions in CA strongly motivate many other states to follow suit. To whatever extent they do, that's mostly covered by the ZEV Mandates indicator.
  • Fuel Economy Regulations: The difference between the percentiles is again pretty small, so I'm rating the impact as very minimally positive.
  • EV Employment: This seems like it would be more driven by increasing EV adoption, not the other way around. Though I could see there being a minor positive feedback loop happening here in the second half of this decade, i.e. more employment in the EV sector slightly increasing the rate of adoption in later years.
  • CO2 Emissions: Again, this would probably be affected more by increasing EVs and not vice versa. While a lot of people would buy an EV with something like "climate-friendliness" being one of their major reasons for doing so, I don't actually see US CO2 emissions specifically being the thing that swings a lot of customers over. Government might care more, but I don't see them acting drastically differently with a ~10% counterfactual difference in emissions.
